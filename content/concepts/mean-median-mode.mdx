## 1. One question, three answers

Someone asks you what an average HDB flat costs in Singapore. You pulled some data from [data.gov.sg](https://data.gov.sg/datasets/d_8b84c4ee58e3cfc0ece0d773c8ca6abc/view) and
found 12,243 recorded
sales data points in front of you, ranging from \$238,888 to \$1,728,000. Reading
out all twelve thousand is not an answer. The question is really asking you
to compress the whole data into one number that deserves the word _average_.

That word hides a choice. There are at least three defensible one-number
answers, and on this dataset they do not agree:

| Metric | Value | The question it actually answers                            |
| ------ | --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Mean   | \$659,717             | Where is the balance point of all the prices?               |
| Median | \$630,000             | What price splits the dataset into two equal halves? |
| Mode   | 4-room (44% of sales) | Which kind of sale happens most often?                      |

None of these is wrong, and none of them is the whole truth. The rest of
these notes develops what each one optimizes, when they disagree, and what
the disagreement tells you. Before summarizing the data, let's play around and explore with the dataset below.

## 2. The mean

The mean of values $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$ is defined as the sum divided by
the number of values:

$$
\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i
$$

For the 12,243 sale prices, $\bar{x}$ is \$659,717 ie. if the total
money that changed hands in six months had been split equally across every
transaction, each flat would have cost \$659,717.

The mean is also the entry point to a family you will keep meeting. The
$k$-th raw _sample moment_ of the data is

$$
m_k = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^k
$$

and setting $k = 1$ recovers the mean exactly, so the mean is the first
sample moment. Variance is the second moment, and skewness, the asymmetry that drives everything in
[section 6](#6-differing-averages), is measured
as the third moment.

**Intuition.**  Imagine placing all 12,243 prices along a ruler, with each price represented by an equal weight. The mean,
$\bar{x}$, is the point at which the ruler balances. Prices farther from the pivot exert a stronger pull, and at the mean, the pulls from both sides cancel out exactly.

This balancing interpretation also reveals something deeper: the mean is not merely a formula, it is the solution to an optimization problem.

Suppose you must summarize all the prices using a single value $c$. For each price $x_i$, your error is the distance $x_i-c$. If the penalty for an error is its squared distance, then the total penalty is

$$
E(c) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - c)^2
$$

Differentiate with respect to $c$ and set the derivative to zero:

$$
\frac{dE}{dc} = -2 \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - c) = 0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
c = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i = \bar{x}
$$

The second derivative is $2n > 0$, so this point is indeed a minimum. Therefore, the mean is the single value that minimizes the total squared error across all observations.

Keep this connection in mind. When you later encounter loss functions and linear regression, squared-error minimization will appear again. In a sense, these models extend the same idea: they search for predictions that behave like conditional means, given the available features.

A useful extension follows directly from this interpretation. If a dataset is divided into groups and you know each group’s mean, the overall mean is not the simple average of those group means unless the groups are the same size. Instead, it is their size-weighted average:

$$
\bar{x} = \sum_{g} \frac{n_g}{n} \, \bar{x}_g
$$

On our dataset the four big flat types have means of \$472,616 (3-room,
2,929 sales), \$677,754 (4-room, 5,385 sales), \$790,206 (5-room, 2,795
sales), and \$926,546 (executive, 742 sales). The overall mean leans toward
the 4-room figure because 4-room sales outnumber executive sales roughly
seven to one. The weights matter, and [section 8](#8-limitations)
shows how badly things go when they are ignored.

## 3. Effect of outliers on the mean

Zoom in on one small, real slice: the 29 resales of 3-room flats in Geylang
in April 2026. Twenty-two of them sold between \$292,000 and \$445,000.
The other seven sold between \$617,000 and \$850,000.

![Dot plot of all 29 Geylang 3-room resales in April 2026, with the mean line far to the right of the dashed median line](/images/concepts/mean-median-mode/geylang-strip.png)

_Figure 1. One month, one town, one flat type. The mean is \$447,433, the
median \$358,788. Both are honest computations on the same 29 sales._

The mean of these 29 sales is \$447,433. Read that against the picture: 22
of the 29 flats, more than three quarters, sold for _less_ than the
"average" price.  The seven
expensive sales pulled the balance point up and away from the crowd.

Why exactly does that happen? Because every point owns an equal share of
the mean. Take the derivative of $\bar{x}$ with respect to any single
observation $x_j$:

$$
\frac{\partial \bar{x}}{\partial x_j} = \frac{1}{n}
$$

Move any one sale by one dollar and the mean moves by $1/n$ dollars,
_no matter which sale it is and no matter how extreme it already was_.

The animation moves one sale by \$750,000. With nine sales on the line, the

## 4. The median

Sort the values in increasing order and write them $x_{(1)} \le x_{(2)}
\le \dots \le x_{(n)}$. The median is the middle order statistic: 
the point at which the number of points to the left is equal to the number of points to the right.
When $n$ is odd, the median is the middle data point $x_{((n+1)/2)}$ and when $n$ is even, the median is technically
any value in between $x_{(n/2)}$ and $x_{(n/2)+1}$ but by convention, we take the average of these two values.

For the 29 Geylang sales the median is the 15th sorted
price, \$358,788. For all 12,243 sales it is \$630,000: half the flats that
changed hands this half-year went for \$630,000 or less.

The median also solves the same optimization problem, but the penalty here is the absolute distance instead of the squared distance:

$$
A(c) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lvert x_i - c \rvert
$$

**Intuition.** Stand at some guess $c$
with $L$ data points strictly to your left and $R$ strictly to your right.
Step right by a small $\delta$. You walk $\delta$ _away_ from every point
on the left and $\delta$ _toward_ every point on the right, so the total
penalty changes by $\delta(L - R)$. Whenever $R > L$, stepping right helps.
Whenever $L > R$, stepping left helps. You stop improving only where the
counts balance, which is the middle of the sorted list. The median is the
one guess that minimizes total absolute error.

Visualising the two penalty functions side by side:

![Two curves through zero, the squared penalty rising steeply and the absolute penalty rising linearly](/images/concepts/mean-median-mode/penalty-curves.png)

_Figure 3. Triple the error and absolute loss triples while squared loss increases ninefolds.
The mean must shift towards outliers to reduce this loss_

Statisticians make this notion of robustness precise using the _breakdown point_, which measures an estimator's robustness by
identifying the maximum proportion of outliers that it can handle before producing arbitrarily inaccurate results. As we have observed, 
one outlier can greatly shift the mean value so its breakdown point is ${1/n}$. The breakdown point of the median is
0.5 as up to 50% of the data can be arbitrarily large or small without corrupting the median's accuracy.

In practice, a common compromise is 
the trimmed mean, which discards a small percentage of the smallest and largest observations 
before averaging the rest. Dropping the most extreme 5% of our price data on each side gives \$647,311, 
which lies between the median (\$630,000) and the raw mean ($659,717), exactly as expected.

## 5. The mode

The mode refers to the most frequently occurring value. Unlike the mean and median, it also works for categorical data, where there is no meaningful notion of a "middle" or a "balance point." There is no average flat type or median town, but there is certainly a most common one. In our data, the modal flat type is the 4-room flat, accounting for 5,385 of the 12,243 sales (44%), while the modal town (most sales) is Tampines.

For continuous variables such as price however, the exact mode can be misleading. Among the 12,243 sales, there are only 1,425 distinct prices, and the most common price is exactly \$650,000, appearing 152 times. This does not mean \$650,000 is the "centre" of the market. Instead, it partly reflects the tendency to negotiate and record prices at round numbers. The raw mode may therefore capture features of the recording process rather than the overall shape of the distribution.

A better approach is to group nearby prices into intervals before looking for the most common value. Using \$25,000-wide bins, the modal interval is \$600,000–\$625,000, containing 746 sales. This binned mode corresponds to the highest peak in Figure 4 and provides a much more meaningful notion of a "typical" price.

The mode is especially useful for identifying multiple peaks. In such cases, the mean and median may conceal important groups within the data, while the histogram shows where observations actually cluster.

## 6. Differing 'averages'

Here is the full distribution of all 12,243 prices, with both summaries
drawn in.

![Right-skewed histogram of all resale prices with the mean line to the right of the median line](/images/concepts/mean-median-mode/price-histogram.png)

_Figure 4. The mean of the full dataset sits \$29,717 above the median, and
56.7% of all sales were below the "average" price._

The mean is 4.7% higher than the median because the distribution has a long upper tail. Prices start near \$240,000, but they extend far higher, with 902 sales, or 7.4% of the total, exceeding \$1 million. These expensive flats pull the mean upward, while the median remains closer to where most sales are concentrated.

This is a common sign of right skew: many observations lie toward the lower end, while a smaller number of large values stretch the distribution to the right. Income, house prices, hospital stays, and website session times often have this shape, which is why the median is frequently reported alongside the mean.

It also shows why the word “average” can be ambiguous. In this dataset, both of the following statements are true:

* The mean resale price is \$659,717.
* Half of all resale flats cost at most \$630,000.

They describe the same data, but they emphasize different parts of the distribution.

The problem becomes more serious when the data contains more than one distinct group. A single measure of centre works best when the distribution has one meaningful centre. When two different markets are combined, neither the mean nor the median may represent the data well:


![Histogram of Woodlands and Queenstown 4-room sales together, forming two separate humps with the mean and median lines between them](/images/concepts/mean-median-mode/two-towns.png)

_Figure 5. 4-room resales in Woodlands (median \$550,000) and Queenstown
(median \$1,023,000), pooled. The mixture's mean of \$668,548 lands in the
gap where almost nothing sold. Its median of \$575,000 hides inside the
Woodlands cluster and ignores Queenstown entirely._

The mean and median are misleading here in different ways. The mean falls between the two clusters, so it represents 
neither a typical Woodlands flat nor a typical Queenstown flat. The median falls 
within the Woodlands cluster because Woodlands accounts for most of the observations, 
but it gives little indication that a second, much more expensive group exists.

When a distribution has two distinct peaks, it is usually more useful to identify what separates the groups and summarize them individually. In this case, the histogram shows that town matters and that a single overall measure of centre does not describe the data well.

## 8. Limitations

**The mean may not reflect a typical value.** In the Geylang data from Section 3, more than three quarters of the flats sold for less than the mean price. This happens because a small number of expensive flats pull the mean upward. When a distribution is skewed, the mean can still be useful, but it should not automatically be interpreted as the value most observations are close to.

**The median may overlook important extremes.** The median is useful because unusually high or low values have little effect on it. However, those values may be important in some situations. A web service can have an acceptable median response time while its slowest requests perform poorly. This is why engineers also monitor percentiles such as p95 and p99 when performance at the upper end matters.

> **Misconception.** “The mean is the correct measure, while the median is only used when the data is messy.”
> 
> The mean and median answer different questions. The mean takes every value into account, while the median describes the middle observation. Which one is more useful depends on the distribution and what you are trying to understand.

> **Misconception.** “The mode is only useful for categorical data.”
> 
> The exact mode is often less useful for continuous variables, but grouping values into intervals can reveal where observations are most concentrated. A histogram can also show whether the data has one main cluster or several distinct peaks, which may not be clear from the mean or median alone.

## 9. The same numbers in code

How to implement mean, mode, median in Python:

Using only native Python:
```python
# finding mean
def find_mean(data): 
  return sum(data) / len(data)

# finding median
def find_median(data):
  if len(data) % 2 == 0:
    median = ((sorted(data))[len(data) // 2] +
              sorted(data)[len(data) // 2 - 1]) / 2
  else:
    median = sorted(data)[len(data) // 2]
  return median

# finding mode
def find_mode(data):
  frequencies = {}
  for item in data:
    frequencies[item] = frequencies.get(item, 0) + 1
  max_freq = max(frequencies.values())
  modes = [key for key, value in frequencies.items() if value == max_freq]
  return modes
```
In Pandas:
```python
import pandas as pd

sales = pd.read_csv("resale-2026-h1.csv")

print(sales["resale_price"].mean())    # 659716.97
print(sales["resale_price"].median())  # 630000.0
print(sales["flat_type"].mode()[0])    # 4 ROOM
```

The groupby version exposes the weighted-mean structure from section 2:

```python
by_type = sales.groupby("flat_type")["resale_price"].agg(["count", "mean"])
overall = (by_type["count"] * by_type["mean"]).sum() / by_type["count"].sum()
# overall == sales["resale_price"].mean(), the weights are the group sizes
```

## 10. What to learn next

A center without a spread is half a summary. The natural next concept is
**Variance and Spread**, which asks how far the data strays from the
center you just chose, and inherits the same squared-versus-absolute
choice you met here. The Geylang tail also deserves its own treatment:
**Outliers** covers when unusual points are errors to remove and when
they are the most informative rows in the dataset. Both build directly on
the mean and median as defined in these notes.
